I am sure many of us so called web developers have at least caught a glimpse of Mark Pilgrim's ultra handy resource Dive Into HTML5. There are plenty of cool snippets and explanations, and ways to utilize the latest features and APIs.

As I was reading through chapter 4: What Does It All Mean?, I was intrigued by the material on the document outline - something that is often overlooked. To get started, take a peak:

Demo Site Demo Site Outline

What Is The Document Outline?

The document outline is the structure of your HTML document. It is a means to visualize and navigate the hierarchy of it's contents, and this is exactly what screen readers use to help the visually impaired, and a well structured document doesn't hurt the search engines either.

In the good 'ol rough and tumble days prior to HTML5, this hierarchy was established with our good friends in the h crew: h1 - h6. A typical outline might work out to something like this:

  1. H1 - Site title
    1. H2 - Article Title
      1. H3 - Article Sub Heading
    2. H2 - Another Article Title

Why Does The Document Outline Matter?

Since the outline is used to visualize the structure of the document, it comes in handy for those who can't see. Having a well structured document outline has huge benefits for people using screen readers, and it makes your content easier to navigate for search engines (which happen to be indexing HTML5 content as we speak). If you can make your site more accessible, why not do it?

The Document Outline Headache

That was it. You were stuck with h1 - h6 for the entire outline of the page. What is so bad about this? The big problem with this approach is it can become exceedingly difficult to manage the outline of your document when you have varying content coming from varying sources.

My favorite widget or plugin for WordPress might use h4 tags or h3 tags depending on the author's intended use or arbitrary use of whatever h tag seems clever at the moment (Come on, we have all been guilty of this at some point).

Lets take two widgets that are on the same level in our layout, both placed neatly in our sidebar, one uses an h3, and the other uses and h4. This is how the outline will look:

  1. H1 - Site title
    1. H2 - Article Title
      1. H3 - Widget One Title
        1. H4 - Widget Two Title
    2. H2 - Another Article Title

That hardly seems clever....

Lets look at another common problem. It is a pretty common practice to use an h2 as a sub-heading or a tagline. You might have something like this in your markup:

{% highlight html linenos %}

Invalid Username

It's a website on the internet

{% endhighlight %}

This h2 you were using for a sub-heading/tagline is now taking up space in your outline! Any h3 below it will look like it belongs to your meaningless tagline!

  1. H1 - Invalid Username
    1. H2 - It's a website on the internet
      1. H3 - Article title

Your document just got harder to make sense of!

Managing The Document Outline

The wonder child of the internet known as HTML5 comes to the rescue with it's sparkling new set of sectioning content elements and our new friend hgroup. How do these fellas help?

Sectioning Content

HTML5 defines four new elements that define the scope of headings and footers. These elements are:

  • aside
  • article
  • nav
  • section

These elements define a whole new scope for heading elements. This totally eradicates the problem of varying content from disparate sources! Check this little bit out from the demo page:

Some Final Thoughts

This new found freedom with your headings and the new possibilities bring up a couple of considerations.

The nav Element

Many of us have been accustomed to something like ul id="nav" or div id="menu" in the past. The nav element is a new element that defines significant blocks of navigation on your site.

You should notice the nav element is a sectioning element. Rather than have an untitled section, it might make sense to take control of this scope defining element so your navigation shows up in your outline:

{% highlight html linenos %}

Navigation

  • Home
  • Blog
  • About
{% endhighlight %}

Object Oriented CSS

Those of you who try to stick to the principles of OOCSS will likely try to follow the rule of no qualified headings. Defining top level styles for h1 - h6 might not be as possible with this new way of doing things. The h1 is no longer defined in the same context every time, and aesthetically you might not want the h1 to appear the same way in every scenario.

The document outline is something worth checking out regularly as you develop. Make sure your outlines make sense, it will have huge benefits in the long run for people using screen readers, and the big search engines are already indexing HTML5 content.
